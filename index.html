Famous Mathematicians

Pythagoras

Pythagoras of Samos (c. 570–495 BCE) was an ancient Greek mathematician and philosopher best known for the Pythagorean Theorem. This theorem states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides:

a² + b² = c²

Pythagoras founded a mathematical school in Croton, where he and his followers studied numbers, music, and the universe. His ideas greatly influenced geometry and number theory.

Carl Friedrich Gauss

Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777–1855) was a German mathematician who made significant contributions to many fields, including number theory, algebra, statistics, and physics. He is often referred to as the "Prince of Mathematicians" due to his remarkable discoveries.

"Mathematics is the queen of the sciences, and number theory is the queen of mathematics."

One of his most famous contributions is the method of Least Squares, which is widely used in data analysis and regression modeling. Gauss also formulated the Gaussian distribution, commonly known as the normal distribution, which is fundamental in statistics.

Leonhard Euler

Leonhard Euler (1707–1783) was a Swiss mathematician and physicist who made significant contributions to many areas of mathematics, including calculus, graph theory, and number theory. He introduced modern mathematical notation, such as the function notation f(x), and played a key role in the development of complex numbers.

"Mathematics is the language in which God has written the universe."

Euler made groundbreaking discoveries in **topology**, **analytic geometry**, and **number theory**. His work on the **Euler formula** e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0 is one of the most famous equations in mathematics. He also developed **Euler’s totient function**, which is fundamental in number theory.

Algebra

Algebra is a branch of mathematics that uses symbols and letters to represent numbers and relationships between them.

Who Created Algebra?

Algebra was developed by the Persian mathematician Al-Khwarizmi in the 9th century. His book, "Al-Kitab al-Mukhtasar fi Hisab al-Jabr wal-Muqabala," introduced systematic methods for solving equations.

How Algebra Works

In algebra, we use variables (like x and y) to represent unknown values and solve for them. For example:

2x + 5 = 15

We can solve for x by isolating it:

2x = 15 - 5

2x = 10

x = 10 ÷ 2

x = 5
